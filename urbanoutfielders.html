URBAN OUTFIELDERS


Competitive Outcome-Predicting Game at Live Baseball Games

https://projects.invisionapp.com/share/WFKLI7OBCSE#/screens/302247042_Sign_Up


Final Project for my COGS 123 Social Computing class. In teams of 4, we came up with a novel social computing experience that was co-located and synchronous. We designed a mobile game for attending fans to predict the outcomes of each at-bat to enhance their ballgame experience.

I did research (observations & interviews), prototyping (MVP, lo-fi, hi-fi), prototype testing, and info architecture. I assisted with branding, competitive analysis, and the Wix landing page.






















Problem


Baseball is considered America's pasttime, yet has dropped in popularity. Its perception as being boring can be attributed to its methodical game flow, long regular season (162 games), and game action not being as free-flowing as basketball (82 games), or physically violent as American football (16 games).

Approach


This app provides fans a way to stay engaged and invested in the game while fostering social interaction with other fans during breaks in action (between at-bats).





Objectives


Provide fans a friendly competition with others by tapping into the competitive human nature of being correct.


Promote social interaction between fans whether it be cheering, friendly trash-talk, or discussion about players/teams.


Allow users to play regardless of baseball knowledge and fandom, as game attendees range from die-hard season ticket holders to casual spectators.





Interviews


Takeaways


  • Game attendees often went in groups with friends or family
  • Disposition during games was laid back
  • Going to game is itself an experience
  • Users became super engaged when game got close and said it was "intense"
  • Used phone during breaks in action
  • Would interact with others around them to discuss game and related topics
  • Don't mind friendly and casual competition




User Needs


Mobile device app. Users used their phones the most during breaks in play.


Easy and quick use. Users need something that takes only 30 seconds max to do because gaps are consistent but short.


Supplemental to viewing experience. Ultimately, it must not interfere with the user's enjoyment of the game itself.





Competitive Analysis






Involves a complex system featuring tokens, points, tickets, and rewards. Relies on external app downloads and ads.






Games have a hierarchy depending on skill levels. Involves cash and has 18+ and 21+ age restrictions.





Involves team creation and a points system that differs by league.






Prototyping


Minimal Viable Prototype


Our initial prototype was a Google Sheets where users made picks for each at-bat. We wanted to test how engaging this social experience would be. We tested on a group of 16 students who were shown a live stream of a baseball game in a classroom. Some of the students were die-hard fans, while some were casual fans and others did not follow baseball at all.












Users enjoyed our minimal viable prototype testing session. Users wanted the ability to compete in small groups (such as their friends). Implementing "Rankings" was another important piece of feedback we received. People wanted to know how well they stacked up against their peers. We incorporated these features in the next iteration of the prototype.



Lo-Fi Prototype


Our second prototype was tested on a larger group of 100 students who were split up in sections, mimicking the effect of being in a friend group. Users were again shown a live stream of a baseball game in a classroom setting.
















Users enjoyed the testing experience due to competing against others and the prototype's ease of use. Some users selected picks ahead of each at-bat and disobeyed instructions, so we made a note to present each pick one-by-one in the next prototype and to add a feature that would lock the user's pick in place after a timer counted down.












Information Architecture



Information Architecture helped me think about the app structure and how we wanted to display content to users. Verifying the user's location at a game and simple identification information (for ranking purposes) would be the first thing presented to the user. Afterwards, a linear path of choosing picks, locking in, and seeing the outcome would be presented; the rankings were made present so that the user could always check up to see how they were faring against their peers.



Final Product



After testing our prototypes and considering our information architecture, we fleshed out an user interface in Sketch and meshed it together in Invision. The main feature we wanted to incorporate for our final design of Urban Outfielders was immediate feedback of results of a pick, visibility of rankings, and real-time updates of changes in rankings.

We wanted users to easily see how they compared to their peers so our initial login/start screen led into the Home screen where users could see their score. We designed the rankings in the top right corner to pulsate when there are changes in ranking.

The countdown timer provides feedback to users to pick and choose a prediction for the player up to bat next before locking in thier choice. This empowers users to switch back and forth between picks and to socialize with friends and peers at the game as they find out whether or not their pick was correct.
